Forex Trading. Technology and Techniques

Forex trading professionals use various modern methods to forecast financial markets and to get better trading results. Some of these methods are based on fundamental analysis of the market, while others rely on a purely technical analysis. Indeed, the majority of traders use technical methods to create market forecasts. This forecasting technique assumes that the historical movements of the market can be a factor in predicting of future market behavior.

Most traders today rely on bankers, stock traders, mutual fund managers and other financial experts; in rare cases, professional traders may fully entrust their investments to their outside experts, who exploit knowledge about patterns in market curves and how geopolitics effects the trading environment. Some of these experts rely solely on algorithmic trading through technical instruments, which can facilitate automated trading. This automated trading requires exceptional knowledge and skill.

The professional trading decision-making process is difficult to formalize. That said, an experienced trader can guess where the market is going.

The challenges of developing automated trading patterns

Some patterns well known to professional traders such as Elliott Waves, channels, Gann Vibrations. These and many more recurring geometrical configurations of curves can express market behavior.

These recurring patterns can express virtually any market behavior. In mathematical terms, the self-similar structures are multifractal structures that are geometrically similar to each other. However, formalizing these structures in order to forecast the market is an extremely complex task.

The gap between advertised results and real results of automated trading systems

Automated trading experts who can take advantage of recurring patterns they see in the market are often promoted as generating upwards of 400% in profit per month. Many websites state that their systems use the most modern forecasting methods – from the above mentioned wavelet analysis to neural networks.

Unfortunately, the reality is that even if these experts are working efficiently, it is only in testing, or for a short period of time; a few months at best. As of today, it's possible to give only two examples of successful solutions to practical problems of identifying multifractal structures. The first one is methods of fractal compression of image files, proposed in the late 1980s by a British mathematician Michael Barnsley. The second example is used in compression of standard graphic files jpg2000. A practical implementation of similar techniques for efficient financial market analysis is not yet possible.
